Birds of a Feather is an android application. This app was made in a group of 6, including me. The goal of the app was to help students find other students in their current class that they share classes with. The app is easy to use. If it is your first time logging in, then you enter your information, such as name, profile picture, and all your classes that you have either taken in the past or are currenlty taken. After the login step is completed, you can check to see if you share or did share other classes with your current classmates. To check this, simply tap on the start icon, this will use bluetooth to connect with students within 30-40 feet, and will show the user the name of their fellow students if they share atleast one class together, and it will also show the user how many classes they share with their fellow students. The user can then click on one of the students they connected with to see more details about that specific studnet. The user also has an option to wave to that student and that student can wave back to them.

In a team of two, including me, we built a Operating System simular to help us better understand how an Operating System functions. Implemented important functions of an operating system such as: timer interrupt, thread synchronization, and thread joining. Developed a memory management system with demand paging for virtual and physical memory to support multiprogramming. Created system calls for file management such as: open, create, read, write, close, unlink, execute. Overall, our Operating System functioned as expected.

The goal for this project was to build convolutional neural networks to classify images of food belonging to one of twenty different categories. The CNNs were built using convolution layers, max pooling, adaptive average pooling, fully connected layers, and regularization techniques such as drop out. More specifically, a convolutional neural network was trained to recognize pictures of food ranging from garlic bread to cheesecake and chocolate cake. The problem was that given pictures of similarly shaped/colored food (like cheesecake and chocolate cake) it can be hard to distinguish between the two categories. Using a basic CNN architecture, our baseline CNN model was able to get an accuracy of around 45%. After improving the baseline model, our custom CNN model was able to get an accuracy of around 56%. Using transfer learning to train resnet18 and vgg16, we were able to get accuracies of around 80% and 79% respectively.
